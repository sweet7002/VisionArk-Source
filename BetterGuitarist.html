How To Statistically Be a Better Guitarist

If you’ve yet to play guitar, this is the perfect place to start. As someone with experience in both guitar and music theory, I thought I’d suggest a few ways to become a better guitarist right from the moment you begin.


For starters, when it comes to buying a guitar, my biggest recommendation is to choose one you’ll enjoy playing. While not everyone may have the luxury to do this, it’s definitely worth considering.


You’re more likely to use something consistently if you enjoy it, and the same goes for guitars. If you hate playing a guitar, you won’t want to play it. Find one you enjoy.


It took me a while to find the right guitar for me. I started out with an Ibanez-style guitar, which eventually led me to the Fender Telecaster, the guitar I still use years later. If you’re in the financial position to do so, I recommend visiting a guitar store. In the United Kingdom, the Gibson Garage is a great place to start.



At the Gibson Garage, you can try out most of the Gibson models. You’ll quickly realise that despite the price tag, a guitar doesn’t make you a better player, a lesson everyone should learn.



One of my first guitars was an Epiphone Les Paul, which I picked up from the Gibson Garage. Along with the Telecaster, it remains one of my favourites.


I’ve found that playing a guitar I truly respect and enjoy has significantly progressed my learning. I’m much more likely to pick up a guitar I love the sound of rather than one that doesn’t feel right to me.


Next, I suggest writing a list of songs you’d like to learn. This can span various musical genres, and you can write the list on paper or use my personal favourite, Ultimate Guitar.


Ultimate Guitar is a great website and app designed for guitarists using tabs, chords, and sheet music. I prefer Ultimate Guitar because it offers multiple versions of chord sheets, allowing you to find different ways to learn songs you enjoy.


Although I recommend learning the official versions, starting with a few chord shapes and a capo is a good approach. It gives you the incentive to learn the songs without a capo or alternate tuning later.


You can also use Ultimate Guitar as a songbook, making it easy to share which songs you can play. I’ve used Ultimate Guitar as a songbook for years, and it’s genuinely a lifesaver. It not only lets me quickly show people the songs I can play but also helps me get ready to perform songs I commonly like to play.


My second-to-last recommendation is to experiment with different music styles. Many people get bored of their instruments because they’re playing songs that don’t interest them.


If you like a certain musician, find a song you enjoy. Don’t limit yourself to what other guitarists play. If you like Folk music, play it! Don’t restrict yourself to Rock like many guitarists do.


I started out learning simple Rock tracks from artists like Radiohead and Oasis, which led me to discover other artists I preferred playing. If it weren’t for discovering artists like King Krule, I wouldn’t have ventured into tabs and riffs—a path I suggest everyone explores.


Finally, it comes down to learning techniques. Despite my focus on Ultimate Guitar, I recommend using both Ultimate Guitar and other resources.


This could be a music teacher you know personally or a YouTube video. My personal favourite is Marin Music Center on YouTube, which is known for showcasing a variety of guitar songs and how to play them.


With that said, that’s all I have to share in this article. Although this won’t guarantee your success as a guitarist, it will definitely help you overcome some roadblocks in the future.
